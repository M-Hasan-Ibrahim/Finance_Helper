10 Rainbow scale problems

Show model answer

Rainbow hues lack a natural high-to-low order and are not perceptually uniform. They produce artificial bands and boundaries that can look like real changes.

13 Separability

Define separability, explain why it matters, and give separable and non-separable pairs.

Show model answer

Separable channels can be perceived independently. Examples: position + color and shape + color. Less separable pairs include x + y position, which combine into a 2D location, and width + height, which combine into area and shape.

8 Superhero data mapping (6 points)

You are given a data set of the number of people saved and lost by each of the following superheros: Iron Man, Wonderwoman, Luke Cage, and Jessica Jones. It contains the attributes: the superhero, whether the person was lost or saved, and the normalized date in the Marvel Universe when the event happened. Choose two questions and create a single spatial mapping and encoding of these three dimensions that helps answer these questions. Justify your response.

Show answer

Example questions:
1) Which superhero saves more people than they lose?
2) How does the number of saved/lost people change over Marvel Universe time?

Visualization:
Use a small-multiple timeline / diverging bar chart.

What to draw:
- Create one horizontal row for each superhero:
Iron Man, Wonderwoman, Luke Cage, Jessica Jones.
- X-axis = normalized date in the Marvel Universe.
- For each date, draw bars or dots representing events.
- Encode “saved” above the middle line and “lost” below the middle line.
- Use color to distinguish saved vs lost:
saved = green/blue
lost = red
- If many people are saved/lost at the same date, make the bar taller or stack dots.

Tasks supported:
- Compare saved vs lost for one superhero.
- Compare superheroes against each other.
- Find periods where many people were saved or lost.
- Identify outliers, such as one hero losing many people at one date.
- See trends over time.

Justification:
Date is ordered, so horizontal position is a strong encoding. Superhero is categorical, so separating heroes into rows is clear. Saved/lost is categorical/binary, so color and direction above/below baseline work well. Counts are quantitative, so bar height or number of stacked dots can encode amount.

9 Graphical integrity (3 points)

Edward Tufte argues that a visualization should not lie about the data. What are three ways that we have seen for a visualization to lie?

Show answer

1) Misleading or truncated axes.
Example: starting the y-axis at 90 instead of 0 to make a small change look huge.

2) Misleading area/volume/3D encodings.
Example: using circle diameter or 3D volume incorrectly so a value looks much larger than it really is.

3) Removing context / cherry-picking data.
Example: showing only selected years or selected categories that support one conclusion, while hiding the rest.

Other acceptable answers:
- Unequal scale intervals.
- Bad aspect ratio that exaggerates a trend.
- Chartjunk that distracts from the data.
- Using inappropriate visual encodings.

10 Rainbow color scale (2 points)

Describe two problems with the full hue (rainbow) color scale to encode quantitative data.

Show answer

1) Rainbow colors do not have a natural perceptual order, so it is hard to know which color means higher or lower.

2) Rainbow scales are not perceptually uniform. They create artificial boundaries and make some differences look stronger than they really are.

Other acceptable problem:
Rainbow color scales can be bad for colorblind users and may not print well in black and white.

11 Data dimensionality (4 points, 1 point each)

Give an example of a visualization technique for (a) univariate, (b) bivariate, (c) trivariate, and (d) hypervariate data.

Show answer

a) Univariate: histogram.
What to draw:
One variable on the x-axis, frequency/count on the y-axis.

b) Bivariate: scatterplot.
What to draw:
X-axis = variable 1, Y-axis = variable 2, one point per item.

c) Trivariate: bubble chart.
What to draw:
X-axis = variable 1, Y-axis = variable 2, point size or color = variable 3.

d) Hypervariate: parallel coordinates.
What to draw:
Several vertical axes, one per variable. Each data item is a polyline crossing all axes.

Other acceptable hypervariate answer:
Scatterplot matrix.

12 Tree drawings (4 points)

Identify two distinct ways that we have seen in class for drawing a tree. Provide a name and a drawing of each.

Show answer

1) Node-link tree.
What to draw:
Draw one root node at the top, children below it, and lines connecting parent nodes to child nodes.

2) Treemap.
What to draw:
Draw one large rectangle divided into nested smaller rectangles. Each nested rectangle represents a subtree or node.

Other acceptable answers:
- Radial tree: root in the center, children arranged outward.
- Icicle plot: stacked rectangles by hierarchy level.
- Sunburst: circular/radial version of an icicle plot.

13 Separability (3 points)

a) What is the notion of separability of graphical properties (or graphical channels) for marks and why is it important? b) Describe two properties that are separable and two properties that are not separable.

Show answer

a) Separability means that two visual channels can be perceived independently when used together on the same mark.

It is important because if channels are separable, the viewer can read two encoded data dimensions separately. If they are not separable, the dimensions interfere with each other.

b) Separable properties:
- Position and color.
- Shape and color.

Not separable properties:
- X-position and Y-position, because they combine into one perceived 2D location.
- Width and height of a rectangle, because they combine into perceived area/shape.

Other acceptable non-separable examples:
- Red/green and yellow/blue color components.
- Size and area perception.

Describe three analytic tasks seen in class. For each task, sketch a visualisation or interaction that helps demonstrate that task in action.

Show answer

1) Identify.
Meaning:
Find information about one item.

What to draw:
A scatterplot with one point highlighted and a tooltip showing its value/name.

2) Compare.
Meaning:
Compare two or more items.

What to draw:
Two bars sharing the same baseline, or two highlighted points on the same axis.

3) Summarize.
Meaning:
Understand the overall pattern of the whole dataset.

What to draw:
A histogram showing the distribution, or a line chart showing an overall trend.

Other acceptable tasks:

- Locate: known item, unknown location.
- Browse: unknown item, known location.
- Explore: unknown item, unknown location.

15 Visualization critique (6 points)

Critique the visualization shown on the following page. a) Identify one pertinent task for which it is well-suited and one pertinent task for which it is ill-suited. b) Describe one problem with this visualization and how you would fix it. c) Describe one thing the design of this visualisation does well and why.

Show answer

Visualization shown:
A stacked spending visualization showing average annual spending by income group. It has income groups on the horizontal direction and spending categories such as housing, food and beverage, transportation, health care, entertainment, apparel/services, education, pensions, cash contributions, and other.

a) Well-suited task:
It is well-suited for seeing the overall composition of spending across income groups. For example, it helps show that housing, food, and transportation take large parts of spending, and that the proportions change with income.

Ill-suited task:
It is ill-suited for exact comparison of one spending category across income groups, especially categories in the middle of the stack, because they do not share a common baseline.

b) One problem + fix:
Problem:
Stacked areas make it hard to compare categories that are not aligned to the same baseline. For example, comparing health care or entertainment across income groups is difficult.

Fix:
Use small multiples: one aligned line chart or bar chart per spending category. Another fix is to use grouped bars with a common baseline for each category.

Another possible problem:
There are many colors and categories, making the chart visually busy.

Fix:
Reduce the number of categories by grouping small ones into “Other”, or use direct labels and a clearer legend.

Another possible problem:
The rotated income labels are hard to read.

Fix:
Use horizontal labels, more spacing, or a cleaner axis.

c) One thing the design does well:
It uses a stacked part-to-whole layout, so the viewer can quickly see how total spending is divided among categories. It also orders income groups from low to high, which makes trends across income easier to see.
