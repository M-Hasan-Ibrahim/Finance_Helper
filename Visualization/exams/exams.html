8 Game of Thrones visual mapping (6 points)

You are given a data set of episodes from Game of Thrones. For each episode, it contains the characters killed, their affiliation, the number of romantic encounters depicted, the average visual darkness of the image, and the episode's average rating. Choose two questions and create a single spatial mapping and encoding of three of these dimensions that helps answer these questions. Justify your response.

Show answer

Question 1: Do darker episodes tend to have higher or lower ratings?

Question 2: Do episodes with more deaths tend to receive higher ratings?

Visualization to draw: Draw a scatterplot where each point represents one episode.

Encoding:

  • X-axis: average visual darkness of the episode
  • Y-axis: average episode rating
  • Point size: number of characters killed in the episode

Justification: This visualization uses position on the x-axis and y-axis for two quantitative variables, which is effective because position is one of the easiest visual channels to compare accurately. The viewer can quickly see whether darker episodes have better or worse ratings. The size of each point adds the third dimension, showing how many deaths occurred in each episode. Large points near high ratings would suggest that episodes with many deaths are rated highly, while large points near low ratings would suggest the opposite. This single mapping helps answer both questions at the same time.

9 Graphical integrity (3 points)

Edward Tufte argues that a visualization should not lie about the data. a) What are three ways that we have seen for a visualization to lie? b) Choose two and draw an example.

Show answer

a) Three ways a visualization can lie:

  1. Using an inappropriate or truncated scale.
  2. Removing or hiding important context.
  3. Using misleading encodings, such as area or volume that exaggerates differences.

b) Examples to draw:

Example 1: Inappropriate scale. Draw a line chart where the y-axis starts at 90 instead of 0. A small change from 95 to 100 will look very large because the axis is truncated.

Example 2: Misleading encoding. Draw two circles representing values 3 and 6. If the circle for 6 has twice the radius of the circle for 3, its area becomes much more than double, so the visual exaggerates the difference.

Justification: A visualization lies when the visual impression does not match the real data. Bad scales, missing context, and misleading encodings can make small changes look huge or make the reader believe in a false pattern.

10 Rainbow color scale (2 points)

Describe two problems with using the full hue (rainbow) color scale to encode quantitative data.

Show answer
  1. Rainbow colors do not have a natural perceived order. It is not obvious whether red is greater than green or blue, so it is bad for quantitative values.
  2. Rainbow colors are not perceptually smooth. Some color changes look stronger than others, which creates artificial boundaries or banding in the data.

11 Data dimensionality (2 points)

Give an example of a visualization technique for (a) univariate, (b) bivariate, (c) trivariate, and (d) hypervariate data.

Show answer

a) Univariate data: Use a histogram or dot plot showing one variable, for example episode rating only.

b) Bivariate data: Use a scatterplot with two variables, for example x-axis = visual darkness and y-axis = episode rating.

c) Trivariate data: Use a scatterplot with a third visual encoding, for example x-axis = darkness, y-axis = rating, and point size = number of deaths.

d) Hypervariate data: Use parallel coordinates, where each vertical axis is one variable and each data item is drawn as a line crossing all axes.

12 Non-tree graph drawings (4 points)

Identify two distinct ways that we have seen in class for drawing a non-tree graph. Provide a name and a drawing of each.

Show answer

1. Node-link diagram: Draw nodes as dots or circles and draw edges as lines connecting them. This is useful for seeing connections and paths between nodes.

2. Adjacency matrix: Draw a square matrix where rows and columns are nodes. A filled cell at row A and column B means there is an edge between A and B. This is useful for dense graphs and avoids the node-placement problem.

13 Separability (6 points)

a) What is the notion of separability of graphical properties (or graphical channels) for marks and why is it important? b) Describe two properties that are separable and two properties that are not separable.

Show answer

a) Separability means that two visual channels can be perceived independently. It is important because if channels are separable, the reader can correctly read each encoded data variable. If they are not separable, the reader perceives them together as one combined visual effect, which makes the mapping harder to understand.

b) Separable properties:

  1. Position and color hue.
  2. Shape and color hue.

Non-separable properties:

  1. X-position and Y-position, because together they are perceived as one 2D spatial location.
  2. Red, green, and blue color components, because they combine into one perceived color rather than being read independently.

8 Superhero data mapping (6 points)

You are given a data set of the number of people saved and lost by each of the following superheroes: Iron Man, Wonderwoman, Luke Cage, and Jessica Jones. It contains the attributes: the superhero, whether the person was lost or saved, and the normalized date in the Marvel Universe when the event happened. Choose two questions and create a single spatial mapping and encoding of these three dimensions that helps answer these questions. Justify your response.

Show answer

Question 1: Which superhero saves more people and which superhero loses more people?

Question 2: How do saved and lost events change over time for each superhero?

Visualization to draw: Draw a timeline scatterplot.

Encoding:

  • X-axis: normalized date in the Marvel Universe
  • Y-axis: superhero name: Iron Man, Wonderwoman, Luke Cage, Jessica Jones
  • Color: event type, for example green = saved and red = lost
  • Each point: one saved or lost person/event

Justification: This visualization uses position on the x-axis to show time, and position on the y-axis to separate superheroes. Color is used for the categorical variable saved/lost. It helps us compare the heroes and see whether saves or losses happen more often, and also whether these events are clustered at certain periods in time.

8 Star Trek spatial mapping (6 points)

You are given a dataset crew members lost for each of the Star Trek captains and commanders: James T. Kirk, Jean-Luc Picard, Benjamin Sisko, Kathryn Janeway, and Jonathan Archer. It contains the attributes: the commander, the episode number, and the color of each crew member's uniform (red, blue, gold/yellow). Define a spatial mapping for these data and how you would encode these three dimensions. What tasks does your mapping help satisfy? Justify your response.

Show answer

Visualization to draw: Draw a small-multiple timeline, one horizontal row for each captain/commander.

Encoding:

  • X-axis: episode number
  • Y-axis: commander/captain name
  • Color: uniform color of the lost crew member: red, blue, gold/yellow
  • Each small mark/point/square: one crew member lost

Tasks satisfied:

  1. Compare losses across captains.
  2. See when losses happen over episode numbers.
  3. Compare uniform colors lost for each captain.
  4. Detect clusters of many deaths in specific episodes.

Justification: This mapping uses position for commander and episode number, so the viewer can easily compare across captains and follow changes over time. Color is appropriate for uniform color because it is a categorical attribute. Using one mark per lost crew member also makes repeated deaths visible as clusters.

10 Greyscale encoding (2 points)

Describe one advantage and one problem with using a greyscale encoding (e.g. black to white).

Show answer

Advantage: Greyscale has a naturally perceived order from dark to light, so it can work well for ordered or quantitative data.

Problem: It can be hard to distinguish many close grey values, especially with surrounding colors, bad contrast, or printing/display differences.
