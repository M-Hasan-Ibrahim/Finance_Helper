Chapter 6

Digital Eco-design and Responsible Practices

Reducing digital impacts by extending equipment lifetime, limiting unnecessary use, and designing simple services around genuine needs.

1. Environmental Impact of Digital Technology

Digital technology represents approximately 3–4.4% of global greenhouse-gas emissions. Its footprint continues to grow because of streaming, connected devices, cryptocurrencies, data centres, and artificial intelligence.

1995

The average web page weighed around 14 KB.

2026

The average web page weighs approximately 3 MB.

Unused complexity

Around 70% of requested digital features are rarely or never used. Many services are therefore unnecessarily heavy from their initial design.

3. Resource-Intensive Uses

Video and streaming represent approximately 40–80% of Internet traffic. Videoconferencing, cryptocurrencies, and generative AI also require substantial computing resources.

AI consumes energy and water during both training and inference. For popular generative systems, daily user requests may consume much more energy than the initial training phase. The impact therefore depends heavily on the number and complexity of requests.

4. Eco-design and Responsible Digital Technology

Digital eco-design is a continuous-improvement process that reduces a service's environmental impact during design, development, use, and end of life.

Responsible digital technology is broader. It should be:

Eco-designed and energy efficient
Accessible and inclusive
Ethical, secure, and privacy friendly
Sober and useful
Socially, economically, and environmentally sustainable

Low-tech solutions

Low-tech solutions use simpler technologies, fewer resources, and limited energy while still providing the required service.

5. Responsible Individual Practices

01

Keep, protect, and repair devices for longer.

02

Buy refurbished, repairable, and energy-efficient equipment.

03

Use lower video resolutions and avoid unnecessary streaming.

04

Disable video when it is not useful in online meetings.

05

Limit unnecessary emails, attachments, and stored data.

06

Avoid multiplying digital tools and document copies.

07

Switch off devices and network equipment when unused.

08

Question whether a connected device or AI service is genuinely needed.

6. Web Eco-design Practices

CompatibilitySupport slow connections and older devices.
RequestsReduce scripts, trackers, network calls, and external services.
MediaCompress images and video, use WebP, and adapt media to the device.
RestraintAvoid automatic media, unnecessary animation, and unused features.
ProcessingStop processing content that users are not viewing.
StandardsFollow W3C accessibility and performance standards.
End of lifePlan data deletion, feature removal, and service decommissioning.
HostingChoose infrastructure adapted to the service's real scale.

Useful content and features should be prioritized from the beginning rather than optimized only after unnecessary complexity has been built.

Practices may be classified into usage, content, design, frontend, backend, servers, and hardware.

Main Idea

The main priorities are to extend equipment lifetime, reduce unnecessary digital uses, and eco-design services from the beginning. The most sustainable digital service is simple, useful, repairable, and adapted to real needs.
