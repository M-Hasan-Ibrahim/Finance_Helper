Chapter 3

Environmental Impacts and Dynamics of Digital Technology

How digital equipment, infrastructure, growth, and rebound effects interact with climate, resources, water, waste, and ecosystems.

1. Digital Environmental Impacts

Digital technology creates environmental impacts throughout its entire life cycle: manufacturing, use, and end of life.

ClimateGreenhouse-gas emissions and energy demand
ResourcesMetals and mineral extraction
WaterManufacturing, mining, and cooling
PollutionIndustrial pollution and electronic waste
EcosystemsBiodiversity and habitat degradation

Double materiality

Double materiality studies both how digital technology affects the environment and how environmental problems create risks for digital organizations.

2. Metals and Electronic Waste

More than 50 types of metals are required to manufacture digital equipment. The sector depends on strategic resources such as lithium, cobalt, gallium, indium, and rare earth elements.

Mining and refining require large quantities of energy and water. They contribute to pollution, greenhouse-gas emissions, biodiversity loss, and major social problems. As high-quality ores become rarer, extracting the same quantity of metal requires progressively more resources.

Growing demand

Digital technology, renewable energy, and electric transport compete for a limited supply of strategic metals.

Weak circularity

Most metals are poorly recycled, and a large share of electronic waste is not collected at all.

3. Water, Electricity and Climate

Water is used to extract metals, manufacture chips, and cool some data centres. Producing one electronic chip may require around 32 litres of pure water. During droughts, this creates competition with households, agriculture, and other industries.

11%of French electricity consumption in 2022
4%of global greenhouse-gas emissions
70%of French digital emissions from device manufacturing

Mobile traffic, cloud services, and artificial intelligence are increasing demand from networks and data centres. Globally, both manufacturing and use are important. In France, manufacturing dominates because electricity is relatively low carbon.

The environmental impact therefore depends strongly on extending equipment lifetime and limiting unnecessary production.

4. Growth of the Digital System

The digital system is expanding rapidly:

  • The global quantity of data roughly doubles every two years.
  • Data traffic, devices, and infrastructure continue to increase.
  • Computing efficiency improves through Moore's and Koomey's laws.
  • Storage and digital services become cheaper.
  • Digital uses and investments continue to grow.

Efficiency is not enough

Efficiency improvements reduce the energy or cost required for one operation. Total consumption can nevertheless rise when the number of operations grows faster than efficiency improves.

5. The Rebound Effect

The rebound effect, also called the Jevons paradox, occurs when an efficiency improvement makes a technology cheaper or easier to use, increasing consumption and partially or completely cancelling the expected savings.

Efficiency increases→ Costs decrease→ Services become attractive→ Usage increases→ Infrastructure expands

Optimization produces lasting environmental benefits mainly when the system also has limits or constraints on total consumption.

6. Feedback Loops of Digital Growth

R

Digital growth loop

More attractive services lead to more usage, infrastructure, investment, and new services—which generate still more usage.

Infrastructure does not merely respond to existing needs. Once built, companies must make it profitable and therefore encourage new uses. Needs create infrastructure, but infrastructure also creates needs.

Efficiency creates another reinforcing loop: lower costs increase demand and profits, which finance further research and efficiency improvements.

Planned limits

Regulation and digital sufficiency can deliberately constrain growth before resources become critical.

Physical limits

Without planned action, scarcity, rising prices, pollution, and resource shortages eventually constrain the system.

7. Acting on the Digital System

France introduced the REEN law to reduce the environmental footprint of digital technology. Regulation, however, faces economic interests and industry lobbying.

Repair and reuse→ Limit unnecessary growth→ Change incentives→ Set rules→ Change goals and paradigms
  • Extend device lifetime and improve repair and reuse.
  • Limit unnecessary data, equipment, and infrastructure.
  • Change information flows and economic incentives.
  • Establish environmental regulations and constraints.
  • Change the goals and business models of the digital sector.
  • Question the belief that unlimited digital growth is desirable.

The most effective actions change the system's rules, objectives, and underlying paradigms rather than only improving technical efficiency.

Main Idea

Digital technology depends on large quantities of metals, water, energy, and infrastructure. Efficiency alone cannot reduce its total footprint because it often encourages more usage. A sustainable digital system requires sufficiency, regulation, and careful consideration of which digital services are genuinely useful.
