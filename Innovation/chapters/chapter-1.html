Chapter 1

Climate and Energy

The causes of climate change, the responses it requires, and the central role of energy in the low-carbon transition.

1. Climate and Greenhouse Gases

Weather describes short-term atmospheric conditions, while climate describes long-term averages and variations.

Greenhouse gases absorb heat emitted by Earth. Human activities increase their concentration in the atmosphere, strengthening the greenhouse effect and causing global warming. The main greenhouse gases are carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), nitrous oxide (N₂O), and industrial gases.

CO₂-equivalent warming effects

The effects of different greenhouse gases are compared using CO₂ equivalent, written CO₂e.

2. Emissions and Climate Change

In 2023, global emissions reached approximately 54 GtCO₂e. CO₂ remains in the atmosphere for a very long time and therefore accumulates. Reducing emissions only slows the rate of warming; stabilizing temperatures requires reaching net-zero emissions.

Countries can be compared in several ways:

  • Current total emissions
  • Emissions per person
  • Cumulative historical emissions

The Paris Agreement

The agreement aims to limit global warming to between 1.5°C and 2°C. Even an additional 0.5°C substantially increases heatwaves, fires, floods, ice melting, sea-level rise, and biodiversity loss.

Climate feedback loops can amplify warming and may trigger irreversible tipping points.

3. Responding to Climate Change

Mitigation

Reducing greenhouse-gas emissions or increasing carbon sinks.

Adaptation

Preparing societies and infrastructure for climate consequences.

Resilience

Maintaining and reorganizing systems during disruptions.

France's preparation trajectory

2030+2°C → 2050+2.7°C → 2100+4°C

The carbon budget is the remaining amount of CO₂ humanity can emit while retaining a chance of staying below a temperature target. The objective for 2050 is approximately 2 tCO₂e per person per year.

4. Energy and Its Transformations

Energy use is a major source of greenhouse-gas emissions. Primary energy sources can be grouped into three categories:

  • Fossil: coal, oil, and natural gas
  • Low-carbon non-renewable: nuclear energy
  • Renewable: solar, wind, geothermal, and biomass

The energy chain

Primary energy→ Secondary energy→ Final energy→ Useful energy

Energy is lost during transformation, transportation, and use. Electricity and hydrogen are energy carriers, not primary energy sources.

5. Energy Transition

Historically, humanity has mostly added new energy sources instead of replacing old ones. Fossil fuels still represent approximately 76% of global energy consumption.

Electricity's carbon impact depends on how it is produced. France has relatively low-carbon electricity because it relies mainly on nuclear power, while electricity systems using more coal and gas are more carbon intensive.

Low-carbon electricity and electrification are necessary, but they are not sufficient. Energy production also affects resources, land, waste, and infrastructure.

Cleaner energy+ Efficiency+ Sufficiency

Energy sufficiency means reducing unnecessary consumption and focusing energy use on useful needs.

Chapter Takeaway

Stabilizing the climate requires net-zero emissions, not merely slower emissions growth. Because energy is central to global emissions, the transition must combine low-carbon production, electrification, efficiency, and sufficiency while also strengthening adaptation and resilience.
