Chapter 4

Measuring the Environmental Footprint of IT

Choosing appropriate boundaries, assumptions, and methods to measure impacts and identify actions that physically reduce them.

1. Main Measurement Methods

Carbon Footprint

Measures an organization's greenhouse-gas emissions and supports reporting and reduction strategies.

GHG Protocol

Provides the international standard used for organizational carbon accounting.

Life Cycle Assessment

Evaluates several environmental impacts of a product or service throughout its life cycle.

Carbon accounting is useful for organizational reporting. Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is better suited to comparing products, services, or technical solutions.

2. Emission Scopes

Scope 1Direct emissions from fuel combustion, backup generators, and refrigerant leaks.
Scope 2Indirect emissions from purchased electricity.
Scope 3Other indirect emissions, including manufacturing, transport, disposal, cloud, and external IT services.

For IT systems, equipment manufacturing is generally Scope 3, while electricity consumption is Scope 2. External IT and cloud services are also Scope 3.

3. Measuring Electricity Emissions

Location-based

Uses the average electricity mix of the region where the electricity is physically consumed.

Market-based

Uses the supplier's electricity contract or renewable-energy certificates.

Why the distinction matters

Buying certificates does not necessarily create new renewable production or provide renewable electricity at the same place and time. Location-based measurement therefore better represents the electricity physically consumed.

4. Life Cycle Assessment

LCA studies every stage from raw-material extraction through manufacturing, transport, use, and end of life.

Raw materials→ Manufacturing→ Transport→ Use→ End of life

It can examine climate change, resource depletion, water and energy consumption, human toxicity, and ecosystem toxicity.

Functional unit

An LCA must define the exact service being studied, such as “reading a 300-page novel” or “watching a film for one hour.”

Results depend heavily on assumptions about device type, lifespan, country, network, and usage. This explains why estimates for an action such as sending an email can vary greatly.

5. Attributional and Consequential Approaches

Attributional analysis

Assigns each use a share of existing infrastructure emissions. It primarily answers an accounting question.

Consequential analysis

Measures emissions directly caused or avoided by a change in usage. It primarily answers a decision question.

Sending one fewer email rarely changes the number of servers or devices. Its immediate avoided impact is therefore very small, even if attributional accounting assigns the email a share of infrastructure emissions.

The method must match the question: reporting total impacts or identifying actions that physically reduce them.

6. Top-Down and Bottom-Up Measurement

Top-down

Uses expenses, general ratios, location, or total consumption. It is quick but less precise.

Bottom-up

Builds a detailed inventory of devices, infrastructure, electricity, manufacturing impacts, and lifetimes. It is more accurate but data intensive.

Annual emissions = Manufacturing impact ÷ lifetime + Electricity consumption × emission factor

Reliable inventories such as Configuration Management Databases (CMDB) and IT Asset Management databases are essential for bottom-up measurement.

7. Important Reduction Factors

Manufacturing often represents a major share of equipment emissions. Extending device lifetime is therefore one of the strongest reduction actions.

Usage emissions depend on electricity consumption and the country's electricity mix. Efficient equipment can reduce electricity use, but replacing working equipment too early may create more manufacturing emissions than it saves.

Extend equipment lifetime
Repair, reuse, and refurbish
Reduce unnecessary equipment
Rationalize applications and infrastructure
Practise digital sufficiency

8. Data Centres

Data-centre emissions include Scope 1 emissions from generators and refrigerants, Scope 2 emissions from IT equipment and cooling electricity, and Scope 3 emissions from manufacturing and external services.

PUE=Total data-centre energy ÷ IT equipment energy

A Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) close to 1 means that little additional energy is used for cooling and facilities. However, PUE says nothing about manufacturing, water, total consumption, or equipment-renewal policies.

PUEPower usage effectiveness
WUEWater usage effectiveness
CUECarbon usage effectiveness

9. Cloud Measurement

Cloud providers often emphasize efficiency and renewable electricity while disclosing little about hardware manufacturing, replacement rates, water, pollution, and rebound effects.

Cloud emissions can be estimated using:

  • Bills and purchased services.
  • Provider reports and calculators.
  • Server type, location, and data volume.
  • Monetary emission factors.
  • The local electricity mix.

No automatic benefit

Moving to the cloud is not automatically environmentally beneficial. Its complete impact must be measured rather than inferred from carbon-neutrality claims.

Main Idea

Environmental measurement depends heavily on boundaries, assumptions, and methods. It should not merely produce a carbon number: it must help organizations identify physical actions that reduce equipment, energy, and resource consumption.
