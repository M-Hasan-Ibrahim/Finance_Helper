Chapter 5

Deploying a Sustainable IT Strategy

Transforming strategy, purchasing, infrastructure, applications, data, and digital uses through measurement, limits, and strong governance.

1. A Systemic View of the Organization

An organization contains interconnected technical, economic, social, and environmental systems. Its IT system depends on physical resources such as energy, water, and metals.

StrategyInfrastructurePurchasing Sustainable IT ApplicationsDataUses

Sustainable IT must therefore go beyond optimizing websites or asking employees to delete emails. It requires transformation across the whole organization.

2. The Shift Project Framework

The Shift Project proposes six main areas of action:

1Measure the complete IT footprint
2Develop a sustainable digital culture
3Engage suppliers, clients, partners, and institutions
4Create and launch the strategy
5Build a sustainable information system
6Govern and monitor the transition

The transition is a long-term process requiring measurement, awareness, operational tools, and organizational change.

3. Measuring and Creating Awareness

Organizations should establish a complete environmental baseline, evaluate future projects, and monitor reduction objectives.

Employees must understand the major impacts through training, workshops, serious games, and digital-sustainability events. Awareness should focus on high-impact actions—especially reducing equipment production and extending equipment lifetime.

≈ 500 kgCO₂eper SNCF employee annually, around 2% of the company's footprint

4. Responsible Purchasing and Equipment

Large organizations can influence markets by including environmental and social requirements in contracts for hardware, software, hosting, cloud, telecommunications, and consulting.

Buy durable and repairable equipment
Match equipment to real needs
Extend useful lifetime
Reuse components and devices
Develop refurbishment and recycling

SNCF example

SNCF introduced products such as Fairphone and organized collection and second-life programs for thousands of computers and smartphones.

5. Strategy and Governance

Sustainable IT can reduce environmental risks and costs, prepare for regulation, improve reputation, and strengthen long-term competitiveness.

Objectives→ Principles and standards→ Architecture patterns→ Assessment and KPIs→ Team decisions

Governance should provide assessment frameworks, project reviews, and responsible-digital representatives so teams can consider environmental impacts in everyday decisions.

6. Building a Sustainable IT System

The organization should impose limits before environmental constraints impose them.

EquipmentDevices, servers, and virtual machines
DataVolumes and retention periods
SoftwareApplications, frameworks, and libraries
ServicesAccess to resource-intensive uses such as streaming

Software should be useful, maintainable, and efficient. Teams should question business value before development, measure impacts, automate environmental tests, and reuse existing open-source solutions.

Creation→Storage→Processing→Use→Archiving→Deletion

Unnecessary data should neither be created nor stored indefinitely.

7. Infrastructure and Cloud

Organizations should rationalize servers, close obsolete data centres, allocate cloud resources according to actual needs, and regularly review consumption.

Cloud assessment

Evaluate energy, water, equipment renewal, transparency, and environmental commitments—not just price and availability.

FinOps and limits

FinOps can control cost and allocation, but environmental constraints are needed to prevent efficiency gains from causing rebound effects.

Moving to the cloud is not automatically sustainable.

8. Digital Technology Supporting Sustainability

Digital technology can reduce impacts outside IT when it serves a useful environmental purpose. This is often called IT for Green.

Sirius Next

Recommends efficient train speeds to reduce energy consumption and emissions.

Eco-parking

Turns off stationary trains and avoids unnecessary energy consumption.

Net benefit required

The environmental benefits of a digital solution must be compared with the footprint of the digital system required to produce them.

Rail connectivity is difficult because trains travel at high speed, move rapidly between network cells, pass through tunnels, and partly behave like Faraday cages.

Technical improvements alone may create new uses and rebound effects. The organization must also question the service's usefulness and actively manage demand.

Main Idea

A sustainable IT strategy combines measurement, employee awareness, responsible purchasing, durable equipment, ecodesign, data sufficiency, and strong governance. Organizations must control total demand and question the usefulness of digital services instead of relying only on technical efficiency.
