Chapter 2

System Thinking and System Dynamics

Understanding complex problems through interactions, feedback loops, stocks, flows, delays, boundaries, and intervention points.

1. System Thinking

System thinking studies a problem as a whole instead of separating it into isolated parts. It focuses on interactions, feedback loops, and long-term behaviour.

Analytical approach

Divides a system into smaller elements and searches for direct cause-and-effect relationships.

Systemic approach

Studies how all elements interact and collectively influence the system's behaviour.

System thinking helps us:

  • Understand complex and unexpected situations.
  • Identify hidden relationships.
  • Think in loops rather than linear chains.
  • Find effective points of intervention.

2. Systems and Complexity

A system is a group of interconnected elements organized to achieve a purpose. It may contain smaller subsystems and may itself belong to a larger system.

Simple

Few elements and predictable behaviour, such as a bicycle.

Complicated

Many elements controlled by a plan, such as a computer.

Complex

Many interacting, changing elements, such as a company, city, forest, or society.

Emergent behaviour

In a complex system, behaviour results from interactions between the parts. Changing one element can therefore produce indirect or unexpected consequences elsewhere.

3. Causality and Feedback Loops

Causality means that a change in one variable causes a change in another. It must not be confused with correlation, where variables change together without necessarily causing each other.

R

Reinforcing loop

Amplifies change and can create continuous growth or decline.

B

Balancing loop

Opposes change and pushes the system toward equilibrium.

Systems may contain delays between an action and its result. Delays make decisions more difficult and can create unexpected behaviour.

A tipping point is a threshold after which a system rapidly changes from one state to another. The resulting changes may be difficult to reverse.

4. Causal Loop Diagrams

A Causal Loop Diagram (CLD) represents relationships and feedback loops between important variables.

Neutral namesVariables describe quantities without implying direction.
+Both variables change in the same direction.
−Variables change in opposite directions.
R / BReinforcing or balancing feedback loop.
ⅡA delay between cause and effect.

CLDs provide an overview, reveal feedback loops, and support collaborative discussion. However, they are communication tools rather than simulations and can become incomplete, ambiguous, or excessively complex.

5. Stocks and Flows

A stock is a quantity accumulated over time, such as population, money, water, or atmospheric CO₂. A flow is the rate at which something enters or leaves that stock.

Inflow→ Stock→ Outflow
  • Inflows greater than outflows: the stock increases.
  • Outflows greater than inflows: the stock decreases.
  • Equal inflows and outflows: the stock is in dynamic equilibrium.

Stocks change gradually because accumulation takes time. A feedback loop occurs when a stock influences one of its own flows. Savings, for example, generate interest that increases savings, creating reinforcing exponential growth.

Stock-and-flow models can represent behaviour over time and support simulations in economics, health, climate, industry, and resource management.

6. System Boundaries and Intervention

A system has no single natural boundary. Its boundary depends on the question being studied. A digital system, for example, may include physical infrastructure, information systems, economic and social structures, and underlying beliefs or paradigms.

System behaviour is often surprising because:

  • Relationships are nonlinear.
  • Systems contain limits to growth.
  • Delays create late reactions.
  • Individual decisions may harm the whole system.
  • Exponential growth can reach limits very quickly.

Levels of intervention

Parameters→ Information flows→ Rules→ Objectives→ Paradigms

Possible interventions change parameters, information flows, rules, objectives, or structures. The most powerful changes usually affect the system's goals and underlying paradigms, although these are also the most difficult to change.

Main Idea

System thinking helps us understand complex problems by studying interactions, stocks, flows, delays, and feedback loops. Effective solutions require changing the structure or purpose of the whole system, not merely treating isolated symptoms.
