Finance Course Summary Notes

A compact bilingual glossary of the core concepts used in Business Plans and financial statements.

1. Business Plan fundamentals

Business Plan · Plan d’affaires

A spreadsheet document used to present a company’s financial prospects. It normally contains two parts:

  • Modeling · Modélisation: the entrepreneur enters assumptions to calculate future sales, costs, and financing needs.
  • Display · Restitution: the results are presented through graphs and financial statements.

Financial Statements · États financiers

The financial presentation of a Business Plan: the Income Statement, Cash Flow Statement, and Balance Sheet.

Financing Requirement · Besoin de financement

The amount of money a company needs to survive until it becomes profitable and financially self-sufficient.

Fundraising · Levée de fonds

The process of obtaining capital from investors, generally in exchange for newly issued shares and ownership in the company.

Revenues · Chiffre d’affaires

The total value of sales made by the company during a period.

2. The three financial statements

Income Statement · Compte de résultat

Also called Profit & Loss. It presents revenues minus expenses over a period of time. Its final line is Net Income.

Net Income = Total Revenues − Total Expenses

Cash Flow Statement · Tableau de flux de trésorerie

Shows the evolution of company cash over a period and classifies flows into three subtotals:

  • Operating Cash Flow
  • Investing Cash Flow
  • Financing Cash Flow

Balance Sheet · Bilan

Presents assets and liabilities at a specific date. It explains how the company has financed itself and how that funding has been used.

3. Sales, margins, and operating expenses

Direct Costs · Coûts directs

Costs directly linked to the quantity of products or services sold. They are used to calculate Gross Margin.

Gross Margin · Marge brute

What each sale actually generates for the company after direct costs. It finances operating expenses.

Gross Margin = Revenues − Direct Costs

Operating Expenses · Dépenses opérationnelles

Other costs incurred to operate the business, generally fixed. Payroll is often the largest item; others include rent, marketing, fees, and subscriptions.

4. Equity and shareholder concepts

Share Capital · Capital social

The amount invested by partners when the company is created. It appears in Balance Sheet equity and is public information displayed on company documents.

It has no fixed repayment date, and shareholders cannot demand reimbursement of their invested share capital.

Share Premium · Prime d’émission

Created when new shares are sold during fundraising. It is the difference between a share’s economic issue price and its legal nominal value.

Dividends · Dividendes

Cash distributed to shareholders as compensation for the risk and capital they provided.

The distributable amount depends on reserves and accumulated results and cannot exceed the profits legally available for distribution.

5. Investments and amortization

Investments · Investissements

Expenditures made to create a useful and productive asset rather than consume a good or service immediately.

If an item is expected to be used for more than one year, it is generally treated as an investment. Otherwise, it is a standard expense.

Amortization · Amortissements

The accounting recognition of an investment’s use and loss of value. The company records an annual expense across the asset’s useful life.

Annual Amortization = Asset Cost ÷ Useful Life

6. Salaries and social contributions

Salary / Wages · Salaires

Salaries are often the company’s largest expense.

  • Gross salary · Salaire brut: contractual salary before employee deductions.
  • Net salary · Salaire net: the amount actually paid to the employee after deductions.

Social Security Contributions · Charges sociales

Employer contributions paid to public bodies in addition to salary. The course estimate is approximately 40%–45% of the employee’s gross salary.

7. VAT

Value Added Tax · TVA

VAT applies to most goods and services purchased in France and is added to the pre-tax price set by the company. The seller keeps the price excluding VAT and transfers collected VAT to the government.

  • Business customers can generally recover eligible VAT paid on purchases; this is deductible VAT.
  • If deductible VAT exceeds collected VAT, the company may have a VAT credit and can request reimbursement subject to applicable rules.
VAT payable = VAT collected on sales − Deductible VAT on purchases

8. Financial expenses and debt

Financial expenses mainly arise from credit facilities and long-term loans.

Credit Line · Ligne de crédit

A flexible facility that works like an authorized bank overdraft. It is simple to use, but the bank may reduce or suspend it, so it is less secure over time.

Long-term Loan · Emprunt long terme

A longer financing arrangement that takes more time to negotiate. Main repayment structures include:

  • Fixed-payment loan · Emprunt à mensualité constante
  • Constant-amortization loan · Emprunt à amortissement constant
  • Bullet loan · Emprunt in fine

Bullet Loan · Emprunt in fine

The borrower generally pays interest during the loan term and repays the principal at maturity.

9. Corporate Income Tax

Corporate Income Tax · Impôt sur les sociétés

Calculated from profit before tax, found in the Income Statement. Profit before tax equals all revenues minus all expenses recorded before corporate tax.

Corporate Income Tax = Profit Before Tax × Applicable Tax Rate

10. The three uses of a Business Plan

1It creates an opportunity to reflect on and test the company’s Business Model.
2It allows the company to calculate its Financing Requirement and anticipate cash needs.
3It reassures investors and banks by showing the entrepreneur’s seriousness and the project’s path toward medium-term profitability.
