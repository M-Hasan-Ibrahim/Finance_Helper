1. What is valuation? / Qu’est-ce que la valorisation ?

Valuation no longer asks only whether a company made a profit or a loss. It asks a different question: how much is this company worth today?

In this example, the operating business is worth approximately €4.78 million, while the shareholders’ shares are worth approximately €5.32 million.

A company is worth today the future cash it is expected to generate, adjusted for time and risk.

2. Enterprise Value and Equity Value / Valeur d’entreprise et valeur des capitaux propres

Enterprise Value (EV)

Enterprise Value represents the value of the company’s operating business, independently of how it is financed. Imagine the company as a machine that generates cash: EV asks how much that cash-generating machine is worth.

Enterprise Value = value of the company’s operating business.

Equity Value

Equity Value, also called market capitalization for a listed company, represents the value of the shares owned by shareholders.

Equity Value = Enterprise Value + Cash − Financial Debt
€4,778.85k + €538.04k − €0 = €5,316.90k

The shareholders own the value of the operating business plus excess cash, after deducting financial debt.

3. The three valuation methods / Les trois méthodes de valorisation

Asset-based approach

Values what the company owns today, less what it owes. It asks what would remain if the assets were sold and debts were paid.

It considers items such as computers, machines, buildings, cash, and debt. It is useful for liquidation or distressed companies, but may ignore growth and future profits.

Multiples approach

Values the company by comparing it with similar companies in the market.

If comparable businesses trade at 3× Sales and the company has €10 million of sales, the implied value is €30 million.

Discounted Cash Flow

Values the company from its own expected future Free Cash Flows. Each future cash flow is discounted because future money is less valuable and more uncertain.

PER example

PER = Market Capitalization ÷ Net Income = 1,000 ÷ 100 = 10×

A PER of 10 means investors pay €10 for every €1 of annual net income.

4. Discounted Cash Flow method / Méthode des flux de trésorerie actualisés

DCF means Discounted Cash Flow. It asks: how much are the company’s future cash flows worth today?

The method focuses on Free Cash Flow (FCF), not only accounting profit.

Free Cash Flow = cash available for investors after operations and investments.
Forecast FCF→Discount FCF→Add terminal value→Enterprise Value→Equity Value

6. Step 2 — Discount the Free Cash Flows / Étape 2 — Actualiser les flux de trésorerie disponibles

Money received in the future is worth less than money received today because today’s money can be invested, future cash flows are risky, and investors must wait.

The discount rate is the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC). In this example, WACC is 15%.

WACC = the minimum return expected by the investors who finance the company.
Discounted FCFn = FCFn ÷ (1 + WACC)n

7. Step 3 — Calculate Terminal Value / Étape 3 — Calculer la valeur terminale

The company does not stop operating after Year 5. Terminal Value estimates all cash flows generated after the explicit forecast period.

Terminal Value = value at the end of Year 5 of all subsequent future cash flows.

The perpetual growth rate is g = 2%. First calculate Year 6 FCF:

FCF6 = FCF5 × (1 + g) = 803.08 × 1.02 = 819.14

Then apply the Gordon Growth formula:

Terminal Value = FCF6 ÷ (WACC − g)
Terminal Value = 819.14 ÷ (15% − 2%) = 819.14 ÷ 13% = €6,301.07k

Rounding note: the Excel result uses the underlying unrounded forecast values. Recalculating only from the rounded figures displayed on this page can create a difference of a few hundredths.

This €6.301 million value is measured at the end of Year 5, not today.

8. Step 4 — Discount Terminal Value / Étape 4 — Actualiser la valeur terminale

Because Terminal Value is measured at the end of Year 5, it must also be discounted back to today.

Discounted Terminal Value = Terminal Value ÷ (1 + WACC)5
€6,301.07k ÷ 1.155 = €3,132.74k

All cash flows after Year 5 are therefore worth approximately €3.133 million today.

9. Step 5 — Calculate Enterprise Value / Étape 5 — Calculer la valeur d’entreprise

Enterprise Value = PV of Years 1–5 FCF + PV of Terminal Value
€1,646.11k + €3,132.74k = €4,778.85k

The value of the operating business is approximately €4.78 million.

10. Step 6 — Calculate Equity Value / Étape 6 — Calculer la valeur des capitaux propres

Equity Value = Enterprise Value + Cash − Debt
€4,778.85k + €538.04k − €0 = €5,316.90k

The value of the shareholders’ shares is approximately €5.32 million.

12. What to remember for the exam / Ce qu’il faut retenir pour l’examen

  • DCF values a company using future Free Cash Flows.
  • Future money is worth less than money today.
  • WACC is the discount rate used to calculate present value.
  • Terminal Value represents the value after the explicit forecast period.
  • Enterprise Value is the value of the operating business.
  • Equity Value is the value belonging to shareholders.
Discounted FCF = FCF ÷ (1 + WACC)n
Terminal Value = FCF5 × (1 + g) ÷ (WACC − g)
Enterprise Value = Σ Discounted FCF + Discounted Terminal Value
Equity Value = Enterprise Value + Cash − Debt
The company is worth today the sum of all future cash it will generate, discounted because the future is risky and far away.
